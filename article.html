Inside the Fight to Keep Bronzeville's Last Independent Record Store Alive

For 30 years, Dusty Groove has been more than a record store — it's been a gathering place, a community archive, and a refuge for Chicago's music lovers. Now rising rents threaten everything.

MR
Marcus Rivera
June 18, 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  Community
HERO IMAGE — 21:9
Inside Dusty Groove Records on a Friday afternoon. Photo by Camila Ortega.

The smell hits you first — vinyl, dust, decades. Walk into Dusty Groove Records on a weekday afternoon and you'll find three or four people flipping through crates with the quiet focus of archivists. Nobody is in a rush. Nobody is on their phone. The music playing from the back room is always something you've never heard before but immediately need to know.

Owner Ray Delgado started the store in 1994 with a folding table, 200 records, and a handwritten sign. "People said I was crazy," he says, laughing from behind the counter where he's spent more hours than he can count. "A record store in this neighborhood? In '94? But I knew what I had."

"This isn't just a record store. It's where people come when they need to remember who they are."

— Ray Delgado, owner, Dusty Groove Records

What Ray had was an instinct for community. Over three decades, Dusty Groove became something rare — a genuinely local institution. Weekend listening parties. DJ workshops for teenagers who couldn't afford equipment. A wall of local artists whose records he carried before anyone else would. The store has outlasted three economic cycles, two recessions, the rise of streaming, and a global pandemic.

The Rent Notice That Changed Everything

In February, Ray received a letter from his landlord. After 18 years in the same location, his rent would increase by 60% at the next renewal. The new number — $6,800 a month — is more than double what many comparable independent retailers are paying in similar neighborhoods outside the city's most gentrified corridors.

"I sat with that letter for three days," Ray says. "I didn't tell anyone at first. Not my wife, not my staff. I just sat with it."

INLINE IMAGE
Ray Delgado in the back room, where he does most of his buying and sorting. Photo by Camila Ortega.

The neighborhood around the store has changed dramatically over the past decade. What was once a stretch of barbershops, bodegas, and laundromats is now dotted with coffee shops, a boutique fitness studio, and a "curated vintage" shop that opened last spring. Long-time residents call it by the name that has become familiar in neighborhood newsletters and city council meetings: displacement.

A Community Responds

When Ray finally told his regulars about the rent hike, something unexpected happened. Within 48 hours, a mutual aid group had formed. A GoFundMe was live. Three local alderpersons had called. Chicago radio stations picked up the story. The hashtag #SaveDustyGroove was trending locally by the end of the week.

"I was overwhelmed," Ray admits. "I've always thought of the store as a place I run for the community. I didn't realize the community thought of it the same way."

The response speaks to something deeper than nostalgia for a record store. In a city that has seen hundreds of neighborhood anchors close over the past decade — barbershops, community theaters, family-owned restaurants — Dusty Groove has become a symbol. Its survival, many believe, says something about what kind of city Chicago intends to be.

What Comes Next

Ray is currently in negotiation with his landlord and working with the Chicago Independent Business Alliance to explore options including a long-term lease, a potential ownership stake in the building, and a community benefit agreement that would tie any sale of the property to protections for existing tenants.

"I'm not giving up," he says. "I've been here 30 years. I know how to fight." He pauses, then smiles. "And I've got pretty good records to put on while I do it."

If you'd like to support Dusty Groove, visit their GoFundMe or stop by the store at 4731 S. Cottage Grove Ave., Chicago.
